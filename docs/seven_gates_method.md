# The Seven Gates Method

**A systematic approach to excavating biblical concepts — from modern distortion to original meaning.**

---

## Overview

Every Hebrew word in Scripture has traveled through multiple linguistic and cultural layers before reaching the modern reader:

**Hebrew (Tanakh) → Greek (Septuagint) → Latin (Vulgate) → Church Slavonic → Russian (Synodal) → Modern perception**

At each layer, meaning was added, lost, or inverted. The Seven Gates Method strips away these layers one by one, revealing what the original text actually said.

---

## The Seven Gates

| Gate | Name | Question We Ask |
|------|------|-----------------|
| 1 | Illusion | What does the modern reader *think* this word means? |
| 2 | Russian Layer | How did the Synodal translation distort it? |
| 3 | Church Slavonic Layer | What false friends and oppositions entered here? |
| 4 | Greek Layer | How did the Septuagint Hellenize the concept? |
| 5 | Hebrew Root | What is the concrete, imagistic meaning of the root? |
| 6 | Shift Map | What was added, lost, or inverted at each layer? |
| 7 | Restoration | How should we read this word anew? |

---

## Gate 1: Illusion

**What the modern reader thinks the word means.**

We start with the contemporary perception — the automatic, unexamined understanding that a reader brings to the text.

**Example (Torah):**
- Modern reader hears "Law"
- Images: rules, prohibitions, courts, punishment, "Old Testament severity"

**Why this matters:** If we don't name the illusion, we can't dismantle it.

---

## Gate 2: Russian Layer (Synodal Translation)

**How the Synodal translation rendered the word.**

The Synodal translation (1876) is the standard Russian Bible. It inherited distortions from Greek and Slavonic and added its own.

**What we examine:**
- The Russian word chosen as a translation
- Its semantic field in Russian (connotations, associations)
- Specific problems with this rendering

**Example (Torah):**
- Replacement word: *Закон* (Law)
- Semantic field: jurisprudence, coercion, boundary, letter (opposed to spirit)
- Problem: Russian "Закон" comes from *za-kon* — "that which lies beyond/before/at the boundary" — something external, imposed

---

## Gate 3: Church Slavonic Layer

**The Slavonic translation and its influence.**

Church Slavonic (9th century CE) was the liturgical language of Russian Orthodoxy. It shaped how Russians understood Scripture for a millennium.

**What we examine:**
- The Slavonic word
- Peculiarities of Slavonic understanding
- How Slavonic influenced modern Russian perception
- Oppositions introduced (e.g., Law vs. Grace, Old vs. New)

**Example (Torah):**
- Word: *Законъ* (Zakonŭ)
- Not just legal act, but "fatherly heritage," "established order"
- Introduced opposition: Law (Old Testament) vs. Grace (New Testament)

---

## Gate 4: Greek Layer (Septuagint & New Testament)

**The Greek translation that became the bridge to the West.**

The Septuagint (3rd-2nd century BCE) translated Hebrew into Greek for the Alexandrian Jewish community. The New Testament was written in Greek. Both used Greek philosophical categories foreign to Hebrew thought.

**What we examine:**
- The Greek word (transliteration and original)
- Its etymology (what it literally means in Greek)
- Its semantic field in Greek culture
- What Greek added to the Hebrew concept
- What Greek lost or suppressed

**Example (Torah):**
- Greek word: *νόμος* (Nomos)
- Etymology: νέμω — "to distribute," "to shepherd," "to govern"
- Semantic field: custom, civil law, established order
- Added: legalism, external norm, opposition to "nature"
- Lost: personal relationship, path imagery, fatherly instruction

---

## Gate 5: Hebrew Root

**The source — the Hebrew word itself.**

Here we return to the original Hebrew. We ignore all translations and ask: what did this word actually mean to the people who spoke it?

**What we examine:**
- The Hebrew word (with vowels)
- The three-letter root (Shoreš)
- The concrete, imagistic meaning of the root
- The form of the word and what it indicates
- The imagery behind the concept
- Key verses in Tanakh where the meaning is clear

**Example (Torah):**
- Word: *תּוֹרָה* (Torah)
- Root: *ירה* (Yarah) — to throw, to cast, to direct, to instruct
- Form: verbal noun — "direction," "instruction," "guidance"
- Imagery: an arrow shot toward a target, a hand pointing the way, a father instructing his son

**Comparison Table:**

| Greek νόμος | Hebrew תּוֹרָה |
|-------------|---------------|
| External norm | Internal instruction |
| Coercion | Guidance |
| Boundary (what not to do) | Path (where to go) |
| Legal act | Fatherly teaching |
| Letter | Life |

## Gate 7: Restoration

**How to read the word anew.**

We propose a practical way to read the text that restores the original meaning without discarding the translation entirely.

**What we provide:**
- A principle for reading (e.g., "When you see 'law' in the context of Torah, read 'instruction'")
- Examples of restoration (distorted → restored)
- What changes in understanding the text

**Example (Torah):**

**Principle:** When the translation says "law" in the context of Torah, read as "instruction," "teaching," or "path."

**Examples:**

| Distorted | Restored |
|-----------|----------|
| "The law of the Lord is perfect" (Psalm 19:7) | "The instruction of Yahweh is perfect" |
| "Under the law" (Paul) | Not "under Torah's jurisdiction," but "under the principle of works-righteousness" |

**What changes:**
- Torah becomes a gift, not a burden
- "The yoke of Torah" becomes a harness that helps carry the load, not a heavy weight
- Observing Torah becomes a child's response to the Father, not a legal obligation

---

## Why Seven Gates?

- **Gate 1** captures the unexamined assumption
- **Gates 2–4** show the historical layers of distortion
- **Gate 5** returns to the source
- **Gate 6** maps the damage
- **Gate 7** provides a practical path forward

This method works for any biblical concept — not just Torah, but also Chesed, Teshuvah, Tzedakah, Emet, Shalom, Korban, and Brit.

---

## Using This Method on Any Word

1. Start with the modern illusion — write down what you *think* the word means
2. Find the Russian Synodal translation — what word was used?
3. Check the Church Slavonic — what oppositions entered here?
4. Go to the Greek (LXX/NT) — how did the Septuagint render it?
5. Look up the Hebrew root in a lexicon (BDB) — what is the concrete meaning?
6. Create a shift map — what was added/lost at each layer?
7. Propose a restoration — how should we read it?
